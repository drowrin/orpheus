Though both the protagonist and genre have changed, this is a Yakuza game at
heart. As a new start for the series, it is fitting that this game is about
finding a new place for yourself without forgetting your past. It carries
forward the series' relentless hope, stronger than ever before.

# The Review

I am a huge fan of this game. It is not without flaws, but the highs are
extremely high. Especially for their first shot at this kind of game, while
simultaneously redefining the tone of the series for the future, RGG knocked it
out of the park.

## Gameplay

This is the biggest change from previous games. I love the new direction they
are taking the series with this move. I do have some problems with this initial
implementation, but I still enjoy the overall package. Simultaneously, the
gameplay is one of my favorite parts of this game, and also what holds it back
from being a 10/10 for me.

As mentioned, this is a turn-based game. In fact, it's a full JRPG. You've got a
party. They all level up. You equip gear and unlock new jobs. There are spells
and special abilities and summons and more. The works!

I have not yet played any Dragon Quest games, but as I understand it, this
system is heavily inspired by that series. Ichiban's favorite game is Dragon
Quest. I'm not sure which came first. Maybe both at once.

There are a few bits of Yakuza flair on top of the classic JRPG formula. If a
character runs into an object such as a bicycle, crate, or vase while
approaching for an attack, they will incorporate that object into the attack for
extra damage. Sometimes that means kicking it at the target while running to
them. Sometimes that means picking it up and performing an alternate attack
animation. Enemies can do this too.

Additionally, the position of enemies and allies can have a major impact.
Attempting to run through a pack of enemies to attack someone behind them will
cause your character to be interrupted by being punched in the back. AoE attacks
must be strategically placed for maximum effect, but you can only select enemies
as the point of origin, rather than arbitrary spots. This is actually one of the
flaws in this system: positioning is important, but you have no control over it.
All characters aimlessly wander in combat, causing these systems to be little
more than an additional layer of RNG. Though, if you are aware of these
mechanics, they can still play a role in your strategy by giving you
opportunities for bonus damage.

Another bit of Yakuza flavor mixed into this JRPG formula is that timing is
important, in a few ways. Most obviously, there are quick-time events during
attacks, and you can perfect-guard incoming damage. Timing is important even
outside of individual turns. If an enemy gets knocked to the ground, they will
stand back up after a short time. If an ally can perform a basic attack on that
enemy before they finish standing up, they will perform an alternate attack with
significantly boosted damage. On the other hand, attacks can miss if their
animations don't collide with enemies. So targeting an enemy who is currently in
a recovery animation from their last attack could be risky if that animation
makes them perform a kip-up or some other acrobatic move. These things combine
to make combat a dance between carefully selecting your next move, and suddenly
springing to action when opportunity arises.

This game does something that classic JRPGs did quite often, but seems to be
getting less common over time---unless a game is specifically referencing or
influenced by a classic JRPG. That is, unique, powerful, and game-changing
progression from side content. The ability to earn a new spell for a specific
character, a new summon, or a piece of equipment with a build-defining effect,
all from going on an adventure in the world. Not just an XP or currency reward,
a new toy is given directly to you, tied to the story you just experienced. This
makes that content feel way more rewarding, while also making the new toys feel
more significant by giving them story context. This also often happened in the
main story, causing party members to evolve mechanically as part of their
character arcs.

Where progression falls a bit flat for me is the job system. I love a good job
system. This isn't one. I actually love the jobs themselves. This game puts a
huge emphasis on the idea that anyone can make an impact, and it paints mundane
tasks in heroic fantasy colors, celebrating the part everyone plays. With that
in mind, it's awesome that the jobs are ordinary jobs: host/hostess,
construction worker, clerk, and so on. The problem is not that, it's that the
jobs are not fun to level.

Every job has a ludicrously high maximum rank, and job rank contributes to
stats. This is not a job system in which you constantly switch jobs to build
your characters over time---this is a system in which you give each character a
job and never change them again. They clearly _tried_ to make it the former, as
some abilities do transfer between jobs, but the long leveling process and
stat-penalty for switching makes it feel like you are supposed to stick with one
forever. This makes the job system feel incoherent and inconsistent, like they
weren't quite sure what it was supposed to be. Being encouraged to switch jobs
would have been thematically appropriate for the game, so I'm disappointed.

The other disappointing thing about the gameplay is that it is terribly
balanced. It is clear that this was their first attempt at a JRPG, and it was
one they hastily put together. The numbers on a ton of abilities simply don't
make sense. Some cheap, early abilities are stronger than abilities that cost
much more and are unlocked at a higher level. Some characters and jobs simply do
worse damage, with no real way to fix them. Some abilities rely on synergies of
chaining multiple status effects together and then doing a big finishing move,
but basic enemies die to the status effect application attacks, while bosses are
nearly immune to status effects. The build strategy boils down to a very
specific set of aggressive jobs, and spamming consumables. It's not a terribly
interesting sandbox to build within. Still, the combat is dynamic enough that
it's fun to play.

Finally, there are some grind walls. The best I saw someone sum it up was, "The
implication you should grind was not a suggestion, it was a threat." Some main
story missions would jump up 10-15 levels at a time. Fortunately this all
happens after you unlock pretty good grinding options, but I still did not enjoy
the time I spent doing that. I would have preferred the progression be a bit
more natural and smooth.

Despite all my complaints above, I enjoyed playing this game. The best parts
made up for the worst parts. I loved my party members so much that seeing them
perform these cool, unique combat animations was a joy of its own. It also
helped that the game is exceptionally well paced, very rarely lingering too long
on exclusively combat or story sections. I've heard that many of my above
complaints have been addressed in the next game. I'm looking forward to it.

## Visuals

This is a good looking game! Especially during cutscenes. Gameplay sections are
noticeably lower fidelity, but not so much that it diminished the feel of the
game. It's more that the cutscenes really kick things up a notch and make those
moments feel more special. No matter what, this game has excellent art
direction. The style is always consistent and fun. Bits of fantasy poke
into reality in a way that makes the mundane feel magical while making the
magical feel mundane. It's very clever!

I don't have a lot more of interest to say here. You can look at this game and
easily notice that it came out 6 years ago, but the core is there and nothing
jumps out as having aged poorly. RGG have perfected their formula at this point.
Further improvements will be incremental.

## Music

If the identity of the series is changing, of course its music will too. The
soundtrack for this game has a lot more electronic tones than the heavier
rock-focused soundtracks of past Yakuza games. In that way, it takes a page out
of Judgment's book. I am slightly less of a fan of this style, but there are
some heavy hitters in here that I adore. Also, some of these tracks are new
arrangements of series classics---once again, preserving the heart of the
franchise while dressing it in a new style.

The spoilers: Both Goro Majima and Kazuma Kiryu are in this game. Both show up
as Yakuza legends, here to test your resolve. _Have you got what it takes?_
Other characters show up, too, and they are similarly important. However, I want

Kiryu and Majima share parts of a theme: the original main theme of Yakuza 1,
"Receive You." Over the course of the franchise, they've had remixes of this
song in every game, and which part of the song each character focuses on has
diverged. Still, as twin mascots for the series, they share this core theme.

Now, they are no longer protagonists. They are champions with decades of legacy
behind them. They are towering walls to climb, obstacles on the hero's journey
placed there to test if he can handle the true threats ahead. They have stepped
into the shadows as mentors and legends, but their departure from the spotlight
has not diminished their strength.

As mascots of the previous part of the story, not only must Ichiban overcome
them in the game to prove his resolve, this new story must prove that Ichiban is
worthy of becoming the new face of the series.

They each have a new mix of their themes in this game, played during their
respective boss fights. These new themes emphasize their legendary status and
their roles in this new story.

Kiryu's theme is much heavier and more serious. This is the Hero vs the Dragon.
The fight often depicts Kiryu as a literal dragon that Ichiban must slay if he
is to be the destined hero. Ichiban must prove that he is worthy of the Dragon
tattoo on his back.

There is no malice in this song. This is a song of absolute seriousness, deadly
resolve, and unwavering principle. Kiryu would not fight Ichiban out of joy or
anger. He would fight, with nothing held back, only to provide Ichiban a path of
his own ascension, and to knock some sense into him.

This is a song of mentorship and passing of the torch.

I notice that Ichiban does not yet have a similar theme of his own. Not that I
expect him to have a "Receive You" remix, but a theme that follows him and

whole. I wouldn't want them to jump the gun on that, and I'm glad they left that
spot blank for now. It's a meaningful choice, and one that will make his

## Voice Acting

This game started the pattern of recent Yakuza games coming with English dubs at
release. They even have multiple subtitle tracks, one that matches the English
dub exactly and another that is more accurate to the original Japanese audio.
This allows the player to choose their level of localization. I think that's
very nice. There's constant debate online about how far localization should go,
with some preferring a more literal translation and others wanting the most
natural experience possible in their native language and culture. This provides
both.

I played with the original Japanese audio, and the subtitles to match it. This
is equivalent to how all the other games in the series were localized up to this
point, so it would be jarring to mix things up now. Especially since I am already
used to the Japanese voices for many recurring characters, and I'd have to learn
new voices for them in English.

The Japanese voice acting is incredible. There is some serious talent on this
scene. I was especially impressed with the performance of Kazuhiro Nakaya as
Ichiban. He also voiced Akira Nishikiyama, Kiryu's brother and rival from the
first few Yakuza games, and he was excellent there. I'm so glad they gave him a
protagonist role and really let him cut loose. His emotional delivery broke my
heart multiple times throughout this story. He will cry his heart out for a
scene and force yours to harmonize. I think he is a large part of why I love
Ichiban as a protagonist so much.

I'm sure the English dub is good as well. There's some industry heavy hitters in
there that are always good. But I highly recommend the original audio for this
one.

## Story

The writing is _easily_ the best part of this game. That has been true of most
Yakuza games, but it's never been more true than now.

This story made me think of a few things in my own life differently. It has
inspired me to pick up the pieces of some things I thought I gave up on long
ago. It is a profoundly and stubbornly hopeful story. It shows some of the
darkest parts of life, but also how light can be found anywhere. It proclaims
that it's never to late to redefine yourself, to chase your dreams, and to work
to become who you've always wanted to be. It doesn't say it'll be easy, but you
should never give up, regardless.

> > Once you’re at rock bottom, the only way forward is up. But the bottom
> > doesn’t have to be all dark and gloomy. If you can stand and look up, you’ll
> > see the light of hope there.
> 
> --- Ichiban Kasuga

There's something really touching about how the protagonist of a game that is
about finding inspiration to never give up on yourself, is himself obsessed with
a game that he sees as his inspiration for that very idea. Ichiban is the
world's number one (pun intended) Dragon Quest fan. The most pivotal moments in
his life when he finds inspiration to do what he must, are pictured with fantasy
elements mixed in. He sees his life in the game. He sees the game in his life. I
see myself in this game. I see this game in my life. There's something charming
about that, which also deepens the impact of the game's message. The game is
assuring me that it's not silly to find inspiration and courage for yourself, in
a game. Such things can come from many places in our lives. For your party
members and other characters in the game, these life inspirations, the things
that keep us going, are all different. Yet they all intertwine.

> > A hero doesn't just win fights. A hero helps people find their own way to win.
>
> --- Ichiban Kasuga

The game also has a lot to say about how we look down on people in our society.
Guess what? The ultimate antagonists are conservatives. You get to punch
fascists in the nose. It's what we really need these days. But this game also
extends a hand, even to them. When they hit their lowest, they are just like
anyone else at rock bottom. Anyone, truly anyone, has potential to be better.
People lash out because they need something, and we as a society can get that
for them in a less destructive way if they will listen. This game doesn't just
tell you to refuse to give up on yourself, it tells you to refuse to give up on
anyone.

That's hard! All of this is hard! But we're in this together.

> > Even a hero needs a party. You don't have to do this alone, Ichi.
>
> --- Koichi Adachi

# Final Thoughts

**Rating: 9/10**  
_Playtime: 44 hours_

At the time of writing, Ichiban Kasuga is my favorite game protagonist of all
time. It will take a lot to dethrone him. I don't see it happening any time
soon. I feel that strongly about him, and I've only played one of his two games!

This is not a perfect game by any means. It's not even super high on my all-time
ranking. However, it impacted me personally in a way I'll be thinking about for
a long time, and I'm excited to see where this new direction takes the franchise
in the future.
