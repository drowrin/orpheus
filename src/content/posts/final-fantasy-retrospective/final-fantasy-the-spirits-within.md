I'm including anime, books, and movies that are tied to other games in the
series. So I might as well include this one, too. The Spirits Within is not tied
to any mainline entry, but it is an important part of the history of the
franchise. It's also surprisingly influential, for all the issues it has.

You may be surprised to hear that this movie was influential if you haven't seen
it. However, I think the legacy becomes clear pretty much immediately:
[Mass Effect was significantly inspired by Final Fantasy: The Spirits Within.][ref]
Mass Effect was a massive inspiration for the industry after its release. I
think this also means that, more directly, downstream properties inspired by The
Spirits Within include Destiny, Warframe, and the like. I'm not claiming that
this movie was the originator of those ideas, of course but it's an interesting
link in the chain.

[ref]: https://web.archive.org/web/20141228010247/http://www.computerandvideogames.com/309198/bioware-mass-effect-inspired-a-lot-by-final-fantasy-film/

Now, do I recommend that you watch this movie if you enjoyed any of the media
inspired by it? No. Not necessarily, at least. I think it's most interesting as
a piece of history to be analyzed, rather than as a piece of media to consume.
Unfortunately, the artistic vision for this movie was significantly compromised
during its development. The plot was simplified rather harshly to appeal to a
wider audience, leaving only vestigial traces of what it was supposed to be.
Those remnants are even more confusing without the context they were meant to
exist within. The result is a baffling story that never feels sure about what it
wants to be. However, if you're willing to dig a bit deeper and read between the
lines of the Hollywoodization of it all, there are some interesting themes here.

Final Fantasy: The Spirits Within is a tale of life and death. Not of _being
dead_ or _being alive_, but the relationship between those two states and the
transitions between them. There is pain and terror in the creation of new life,
as pregnancy and birth are forms of body horror. There is death in the act of
living, as our cells constantly live an die. Death sustains life, as we eat the
dead to live. We are all composed of the elements of those who came before, so
in us, they live. What is dead may never die, but lives on eternally in memory
and legacy.

I have to get into spoilers to talk about those themes any more. ||This movie
examines the above themes at multiple levels, drawing analogies between our
lives and those of the organisms both larger and smaller than us. The largest of
which is the planet itself, in a similar line of thought to the Gaia hypothesis,
which is referenced repeatedly in the film. The version of this hypothesis, spun
through a fantasy lens, is that each planet is itself a living organism, and the
inhabitants of it are part of its biology in the same way that cells, gut
bacteria, and so on are part of our biology. If the planet is alive, then it can
evolve and procreate. It can also die---and if living things have spirits, so
does it. Indeed, in the world of the film, every living thing has a spirit, from
the planet all the way down to single-cell organisms.||

||That bit about procreation is the important piece that got _mostly_ scrubbed
from the plot. I get it. It's probably the most unconventional part of the
story, but I think it was also very important to the message the movie was
trying to convey. Fortunately, there are enough references to it that the theme
can still be revived from the ashes of the quippy one-liners and unnecessary
action scenes that replaced it. Here's the bird's-eye view: The entire plot is
actually the story of procreation between two planets. In order to integrate the
two halves and form a new whole, a large number of "cells" from the mother
planet must integrate with a single surviving cell from the father planet. Much
like how sperm are perceived as an invasion by the immune system, the alien
spirits are perceived as an invading force by the humans of Gaia. After a
significant struggle of pain that transforms the landscape, a new life is born
alongside hope for the future.||

Once those details are brought to light, many other elements of the film can be
reinterpretted. This becomes less of a weird action movie with some strange and
arbitrary-feeling fantasy elements, and the true themes are revealed. If you
want to dig into this in far, far greater detail, I highly recommend this video
by Resonant Arc: [ What Everyone Missed About Final Fantasy: The Spirits
Within][video]

[video]: https://youtu.be/myjGDRZe7IM?si=alsJapHh7r_HHKov

How is the actual movie? It's fine. It would have been better with less meddling
to fit industry patterns, but I doubt that would have helped it become any less
of a box office flop. The visuals are impressive for the time, and it was
largely marketed on that strength. There are a lot of big-name actors, but they
weren't given terribly good material to work with, and it often feels like they
were directed to flatten their emotions unnecessarily. The music is good, but
rather than having the usual flare and personality of a Final Fantasy
soundtrack, it's an extremely normal, boring movie score. That's emblematic of
the rest: ironically for its title, the "spirit" of Final Fantasy was scrubbed
away.

I give it 6/10 with a heart. I can't call this a good movie, and I can't say I
actually enjoy it all that much---but I enjoy thinking about it, what it was
trying to say, and the legacy it left behind. I'll be thinking about it for a
long time.
