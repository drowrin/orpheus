I feel like a broken record saying this, but I truly wanted to love this game.
Not just once, but twice. I hyped myself up, prepared thoroughly, and felt in my
bones that I was going to enjoy the ride. I was let down.

In 2017, a few months before the release of its Stormblood expansion, I began
playing Final Fantasy XIV with some dear friends. I had played some MMOs, but
never for longer than a brief honeymoon period with each. I had never finished a
Final Fantasy game, though I had tried to start FFVII a few times. Both of these
things meant that I was in unfamiliar territory, but that group of friends was
very motivating. We had planned out who was going to play each role in
high-level parties so that we could coordinate groups against endgame content. I
already loved playing coordinated, cooperative games with these guys. That was
the hook. It was a massive turning point for my taste in games.

Stormblood came out---to mixed reception, but I was loving it. This was my first
time actually playing the latest expansion of an MMO close to its release. I was
hooked. That was when I finally fell in love with Final Fantasy XIV. The story
somehow worked for me where it didn't for others, and motivated me to take a
fresh look at the story I had mostly skimmed through up to that point. It
started to become something that meant more to me than I thought was possible
for games. It became a source of comfort for me when things were difficult. When
school or work stress were really getting to me; when my family was going
through some trouble; when I was struggling to figure out who I really was, deep
inside; this game was there for me.

There's something strange, though likely commonplace, about falling in love with
Final Fantasy XIV before any other Final Fantasy. The MMO stands on its own as a
fantastic game with an incredible story, but it's also built on the legacy of
the rest of the franchise. Most content in the game is a reference to another
game in the series in one way or another. Some are more blatant than others.
Some are just collections of ideas and the spirit of the franchise that has
persisted through all entries of the franchise, disconnected as they are. So, I
was getting a sort of second-hand experience of the rest of the franchise. I
think this is a common experience for FFXIV players. Those rough, vague ideas of
the shape of the franchise were forming in my head, and I wanted more.

Why am I talking about FFXIV in my FFXV review? I think the context is
important: I was primed to like this game. Shortly after I wrapped up the
Stormblood main story, with this budding passion for the franchise and a hunger
for more, Final Fantasy XV released on PC. Perfect timing! Sure, I had heard
that not everyone loved it, for various reasons, but I'd just have to see for
myself. After all, plenty of people disliked FFXIV and even more so the
Stormblood expansion, but I loved those! Plus, this was the Royal Edition, and
I'd heard some claim that many of the issues were already patched. So, awaiting
the release date, I got myself ready. I watched all the trailers, repeatedly. I
watched reviews, combat guides, and so on. I watched the anime and the movie.
There was nothing more, short of spoiling myself, that I could have done to
better prepare. I was hyped. I was aware of the game's flaws, and kept my
expectations low, but I was determined to love it anyways.

It sucked! I found the experience frequently frustrating and rarely fulfilling.
Despite trying my hardest, being maximally biased to enjoy it, and being more
than willing to accept its flaws if I could find something redeeming about
it---I just didn't find anything redeeming about it. I was angry. I didn't play
any DLC. I uninstalled the game, and did not touch it again for 8 years. I
didn't touch any other Final Fantasy game for almost that many, too. I thought,
maybe Final Fantasy XIV was just a fluke.

Still, after this experience, a seed was planted. Over time, I started playing
fewer multiplayer games, factory games, and other genres focused mostly on the
gameplay and mechanics. I started to love the medium of games as an art form. I
loved the way long stories could be paced with sections of gameplay in between
narrative moments. I started to love how the narrative and gameplay could
mingle. I started to love the ways ideas could be expressed through the medium
that were impossible in others. Back before this turning point, a list of my
favorite games would have included things like Overwatch, Factorio, and
Minecraft. These days, after I've changed so much, my favorites are Outer Wilds,
Disco Elysium, and Kingdom Come: Deliverance II. They may as well be opposites.

After a particular set of absolute banger FFXIV expansions, my motivation to
explore the franchise was renewed more than ever before. That's how I started
writing this series. I played through the games in release order. As I did so, I
found myself changing even more. When I initially started this, I dreaded
reaching FFXV at the end of the line. I considered skipping it, as I had
technically already played it. But the more I played the other games, the more I
looked forward to FFXV. After all, if I'd changed so much, maybe I'd like it
this time. Maybe I just wasn't ready, and jumped into the deep end too early
last time. Plus, there had been _even more_ updates since the last time I had
played it. Maybe I had been too harsh the first time. I watched the movie. I
watched the anime. I watched the trailers. This is probably sounding familiar.

I enjoyed it just about as much as my first playthrough. Well, it didn't poison
my enjoyment of the rest of the franchise this time. So it wasn't exactly the
same. Either I'm more resilient now, or I just braced myself better. I think the
problems this game has run deeper than any patches or updates could resolve. It
is a work of art stretched too thin, pulled in too many directions, with input
from too many cooks, that was killed and brought back wrong. The issues are not
bugs or missing content or lackluster features. The issues are structural. It
can't truly be fixed without starting over, but that ship sailed long ago.

If you want to dig into the details on _how_ the structure got twisted out of
shape to this insane degree, I highly recommend this video by The Night Sky
Prince: [The Truth Behind Final Fantasy XV's Broken
Development][ffxv-dev-video] It's an excellent resource that explains things in
an easy to digest way, and he's organized enough that this ridiculously
complicated story was boiled down to only 50 minutes. He said it all better than
I ever could, and I will more than happily lean on his expertise here. I will
occasionally reference the development story while talking about why I think
certain elements of the game ended up so disjointed, but I do not plan to cover
the entire dev history myself.

[ffxv-dev-video]: https://youtu.be/bGKS0q7wl08

TL;DW -- this was supposed to be a trilogy, and it was supposed to be much, much
darker. Due to fears that certain markets would reject the darker elements, all
of the mature and interesting worldbuilding ideas were forcibly removed after
significant effort went into creating them. The team was then cannibalized for
other projects, with all of the original creative leads removed. A new team was
formed to attempt to scrape together something with mass appeal that combines
the ideas of all three games---and oh, we lost a lot of money on this already,
so please aggressively monetize the living hell out of it. It was impossible for
this to turn out well after such terrible mismanagement from the top of the
company. It might as well be sabotage. It's such an own-goal that I would
suspect insurance fraud if such a thing were possible in this industry, but I
think it's just a severe case of incompetence in the executive suite.

This was a hard review to start writing. I finished this game six months ago. My
feelings are complicated and I found this task to be very daunting. Despite all
the disappointment and negative feelings I expressed above, this game holds a
special place in my heart for being the first single-player Final Fantasy game I
completed. I still want to believe in what it set out to accomplish. What hurts
most is the amount of amazing potential that was squandered. I _love_ so many of
the ideas that **were going to** be in the game, had the meddling never
happened. This game carries the phantom pain of amputated dreams. It hurts to
play.

# The Review

The above introduction probably gave this game a pretty bleak outlook, but I
want to start the actual review by saying that there are elements of this game I
thoroughly enjoyed. Some aspects of this game were near flawless for the entire
experience. Some moments impressed and surprised me, standing out as thoughtful
and intentional uses of game design as part of the storytelling. Some things I
was able to interpret quite differently now that my taste has eight more years
of change and refinement behind it. There are things in here that I love. This
would be a much easier review to write if I hated the game.

I want to begin by highlighting those elements.

## Music

This soundtrack is composed by the absolutely fantastic and legendary Yoko
Shimomura. She has written some of the most iconic tracks in gaming. She
composes across the whole spectrum from high energy jams to heart rending,
emotional pieces. You've almost certainly hear something she has written, even
if you've played none of the games she composed for. Even if just in the
background of a video essay or something. She is most famous for her work on the
Street Fighter and Kingdom Hearts series. Surprisingly, despite having worked
with Square Enix for over thirty years, this is the only Final Fantasy game she
has composed for. It's easily among the best soundtracks in the series.

That said, there's something odd about this soundtrack: a significant portion of
it was written nearly a decade before the game's release. Some of the core
themes appeared in trailers for the original version of the game, before
everything was scrapped and rewritten. They don't always fit the tones and
themes of the rest of the game. These tracks are some of the best on this
excellent soundtrack. Probably because they were made for (what would have been)
a better game.

<YouTube posterQuality="max" id="jrhUsgR5S4s" title="OMNIS LACRIMA" />

Let's start with "OMNIS LACRIMA," the theme for optional bosses and challenging
content. Approaching a threat and hearing _those_ opening notes blast out
**instantly** sets the tone. It instills such a strong sense of danger and
escalation right off the bat. It's like the song is telling you it's time for
the real deal. Then the song fades to a more reasonable, but still epic, level
that leaves your brain with a bit more space to cook without being overwhelmed.

Absolutely perfect song for the occasion.

Man, I could listen to that opening on repeat.

<YouTube posterQuality="max" id="qQn6YSYx8-Q" title="Hellfire" />

Let's keep things heavy, with "Hellfire." I _love_ the chaotic, relentless
energy of this song. It really sells the feeling of the heat being turned up and
the desperation and resolve of mortal combat at the divine level. At the same
time, it contains a bit of tragedy that rises to the surface before being burned
away once again by the march of violence. It's an incredibly fitting track for
the fight it backs.

<YouTube posterQuality="max" id="xC_ClxwGnco" title="Somnus" />

Bringing the energy levels back down for an emotional piece, I will next talk
about "Somnus," the main theme of the game. Technically there is a version with
lyrics, but I prefer this one for the quiet intimacy. It is a beautiful tragedy.
It strikes me as separate voices, singing different songs, growing gently ever
closer, until finally there is an explosive revelation that draws them together.
Yet they can only sing the same sad song louder, with more grief, with more
pain. That pain becomes their strength. They _own_ that pain. They own that
fate. It is a song of inevitability, acceptance, and the resolve to do what must
be done.

This theme is woven into many others throughout the game. I love it dearly.

<YouTube posterQuality="max" id="kHdXz6KFCys" title="Somnus Ultima" />

The most powerful version of "Somnus" is fittingly titled "Somnus Ultima." This
is the song at the end of it all. When the inevitable tragedy finally resolves,
and is reclaimed as triumph. It is not any less sad or painful. It is full of
anticipation of that pain and sadness. But there is more beauty in it than ever.
There is pride and celebration of the ultimate sacrifice and the journey that
led to it. It ends, with a note of acceptance, and peace. But it still doesn't
let go of that unsettling sadness. Fantastic piece.

<YouTube posterQuality="max" id="Xkv3HbHBdBI" title="APOCALYPSIS NOCTIS" />

I have to end with what is certainly the best track in the game, and easily
among my favorites in the entire series. This track and its variants all kick so
much ass. The stakes of epic proportions are immediately evident. The will to
overcome something impossible flows from every note of this song. It's a shame
that the gameplay for the sections this song plays never live up to it, but
those are near impossible standards. At least we got some all-time bangers out
of this.

## The Port

This is the next best thing about the game. I wish I could say that spot belongs
to something more exciting, but here we are.

This is legitimately a fantastic PC port. After the pretty nightmarish state
that most of the preceding games were left in on PC, it was refreshing to see
Square Enix clearly prioritize this so much. The settings were good. The
performance was great. I had no issues with crashes or platform specific bugs of
any kind. There's even an optional 4k texture pack available officially through
Steam as DLC---keeping install time low for those with more modest hardware,
while still allowing the high end machines to go crazy. This is something that
many other, more modern games still frequently lack. They were even going to
support modding tools and custom community content. They never quite got to that
before cancelling all ongoing work for the game, but it still means a lot that
they were even considering adding such a thing.

I did still have a few issues, but they were minor and I could work around them
by simply not enabling some optional features. The HDR implementation was the
main offender. This was more of a "wash everything out in white, HDR means
maximum brightness at all times, please wear sunglasses" type of implementation.
There would frequently be so much bright light on screen that text was
impossible to read. The main menu was so bright that I actually got pangs of
pain in my eyes every time I fired up the game. It also led to some weird issues
with light coloring, leading everyone to be a bit over-saturated, with a tint of
red on one side and blue on the other as if I were wearing 3D glasses. None of
this was good. I prefer my HDR to enhance the dark parts of a scene rather than
making the bright parts pop harder. So I disabled it.

Despite the lack of official modding tools, I did install a few mods. Most just
enhanced the visuals a bit here and there. For example, one enabled higher
quality models for distant objects, which recent hardware can handle much better
than that which was available at the time of the game's release. Another did a
similar thing for enabling distant light sources that would otherwise only
activate when you got close, making distant artificial structures stand out much
more clearly at night and making it less jarring to approach them. I installed
one gameplay mod, but I'll talk about that later.

I was glad to have the option to install mods. Between that and the excellent
port quality, I think this is pretty easily the definitive version.

## Voice Acting

I want to praise the voice actors here for a bit. I don't think the _result_ is
very good, but they made a heroic effort against the odds. The material they
were given was not very good, and it seems like the direction was even worse.
Still, they managed to really sell some emotional scenes that would have
completely broken down without their efforts.

I also enjoyed the corniness of it, to a degree. Some lines were goofy and _just
kinda bad_ but I found them endearing in how sincerely they embraced that
goofiness. Ignis interrupting everything to announce he's ["come up with a new
recipeh"](https://youtu.be/ERUx7--rKF4) made me smile every time. Especially
when it was something absurd like:

> **Recipe:**<br/>
> 1x Cup Noodles --> 1x Cup Noodles<br/>
> _(Gladiolus' Favorite)_

I especially feel bad for Robbie Daymond. Prompto's character was entirely
rewritten for the English localization. They completely stopped paying attention
to the original Japanese lines and made up whatever they wanted whenever it was
time for him to speak. Pretty rough. Tons of cringe internet humor was injected
that was severely outdated even when the game came out. Did a guy in my fantasy
RPG just say "hashtag sorry not sorry?" He did. Yes he did. The fact that
Prompto is still an endearing character despite this is a testament to Robbie
Daymond's skill.

This is a short section. I don't actually have much to say, but I wanted to
shout out the voice actors somewhere in here. They really did a lot with what
they were given.

## Visuals

Game looks good. End of section.

No, I do have a little bit more to say. There's a reason I didn't use
"fantastic" or "amazing" or any other, stronger adjective.

I've seen many people claim that this game looks so good that it holds up even
by today's standard. I would not go that far. Not even close. By today's
standards, it's pretty sub-par. A lot of visual elements aged very poorly, and
some details are a dead give away of what era of games it released in. But
that's fine---it came out a decade ago. It looks very good for that time, but
not exceptionally so. Games were already looking pretty fantastic at that point,
and funneling massive amounts of budget into a game to make it look like all the
other high budget games is no longer impressive to me. I think the reason this
game has such a reputation for looking amazing is that it was played
predominantly by JRPG gamers who were used to much, much lower fidelity games in
their genre. For that genre, this blows the competition of the time out of the
water.

## Gameplay

I need to break this section down into sub-sections, but I'll start by saying
that this gameplay was straight up unenjoyable for me. It has its defenders, and
I don't think they are wrong. The particular way this game was designed is just
_so_ antithetical to the way I enjoy games. Almost every single gameplay feature
actively works against all the others at worst, or feels arbitrary and pointless
at best. It's a pile of patches and band-aid fixes on top of other patches and
band-aid fixes, roughly in the shape of a game.

There is one thing that I absolutely _have_ to talk about, but doesn't quite fit
into the other categories: This game is **obsessed** with slowing the player
down for seemingly no reason. This is one of those games which has a stamina bar
that is completely trivial in combat, but extremely punishing while you're just
trying to move around in the world. That's a very bad combination with a large,
empty open world that requires you to run around constantly.

The thing that seemed so pointless that it made me actually angry was the gas
pumping animation. First of all, having a fuel meter for the car doesn't
actually add anything interesting to the game. It's just more busywork. But they
had to make it worse than that. Instead of just hitting up a merchant or maybe
pressing a button upon parking in a gas station, you have to actually go through
a whole animation of gas being pumped, Noctis lazily getting up from his
position nearby, slowly taking the pump out of the car, slowly putting it away,
and then finally slowly turning back to the world so you can take control again.
This is an action that needs to be taken countless times throughout the game,
for a task that adds nothing to the game. Yet it has a longer animation than
what we got for what where supposed to be emotional scenes.

This philosophy extends to essentially the whole game. You can feel the game
dragging you into molasses with every button press. Everything has way too many
systems, takes way too many button presses, and takes way too long. If basic
gameplay animations were skippable and the game never slowed your movement down,
I believe the game would be about 7 hours shorter. It's _awful._

### Combat

This is likely the most infamous element of this game. Hold button to attack.
Hold button to dodge. It's obviously a bit more complicated than that, but you
barely need to do anything more than that to win the game.

You can do more, if you want. There are directional inputs and interesting
combos you can pull off by switching between the many different weapons and
attack animations in the game. You can exploit enemy weaknesses. You can break
parts off of monsters like Monster Hunter to alter the fight. You can trigger
parries and teamwork attacks and command your allies to perform special moves.
From the sound of it, this is actually a very deep and detailed game, right?

Well, the game teaches you almost none of this. You have to be told about it
online or discover small bits and pieces of it yourself. Even then, none of this
actually helps you complete the game any faster or any easier. It all amounts to
only a small bit of extra expression from the player---a bit of flair. It
_would_ be an optional flex, but none of these things are particularly
difficult, either. Since the game doesn't teach you about them, the player can't
be relied upon to use these systems to pass encounters, so none of the systems
are ever required, nor do they allow you to get ahead of the curve by engaging
with them. All of these things are entirely superfluous.

I don't like complexity being optional. In my mind, if a game does not require
me to master a system in order to tackle its challenges, that system does not
exist. It nets a maximum of zero points with me for its inclusion. More often
than not, such systems actually take away from the experience quite severely. I
strongly value elegant and lean designs that operate like well-oiled machines
with every facet performing a necessary role to maximum efficacy.

The biggest symptom _and cause_ of this system crumbling in on itself is that
you can pause the action combat at literally any time to enter a menu and use
consumables. This takes literally all stakes out of any possible situation. All
you need to do is stock up on the _nearly free_ potions and it will be
impossible for you to hit a game over screen. Your allies will even intervene,
picking you and each other back up whenever you fall.

I imagine they added this potion feature at the very last minute, when they
realized that the other systems just weren't working out as a cohesive whole.
Players could not reliably win encounters with the incredibly fickle tools the
game provides, and enemies could dish out attacks that are seemingly
unavoidable. It's like they designed this to be an attrition-focused turn-based
game, where you don't actually have agency in whether or not you get hit by
attacks. It's the enemy's turn, so you just get hit. But this is an action game.
So it feels _really bad_ to be given a bunch of defensive tools that are **by
design** not good enough to actually avoid damage.

Not only does this potion system completely destroy any sense of tension the
game could have had, it also ensures that players will be spending the majority
of their combat experience in menus repeatedly selecting the same potion over
and over again, pausing the action so much that it no longer feels like action.
This problem gets exponentially worse as the game goes on, as the enemies get
tougher, but the party doesn't change all that much.

The weapon system also sucks. There are cool animations for every weapon, and
they all feel fun to use in a tactile way, but the mechanics around using them
are exceptionally bad. Every enemy has a set of weaknesses and resistances. You
can learn this information by guessing and checking, cycling through your
equipped weapons. You can learn it in some other ways too, but they are somehow
even worse, so I won't spend time on them. The problem is, there are eight
different base weapon types, and you can equip four at a time. In fact, you may
equip even less, due to the way the equipment system works. I spent most of the
game with only a single weapon equipped. So, even in the best case, you only
reach half the possible weakness coverage. Instead of actually _doing the fun
combat thing,_ you're supposed to waste time guessing and checking and maybe
never even being rewarded for it. Meanwhile, simply mashing the same single
weapon in enemy faces will eventually kill them, and not even that much slower.
So why bother including this system at all?

Further, all the most interesting and unique weapons in the game drain your
health when you use them. However, they grant you stat bonuses just for
equipping them. There are two problems with this. First, this directly
encourages players _not_ to use the coolest stuff in the game, when I think the
game should be doing the exact opposite. Second, all this penalty **actually**
amounts to is spending more time in menus popping potions. What was potentially
the coolest stuff in the game has been reduced to actually directly making the
gameplay experience worse, due to this one quirky feature that has no reason to
exist. It feels like a random idea pitched by a manger that got slapped into the
game without any thought.

The royal edition brought with it the ability to play as your party members
during combat. They have unique mechanics and playstyles, though none are very
complex or interesting. I enjoyed Gladiolus' playstyle the most, as it was full
of big, high-energy defensive options and counterattacks. I also just love big
swords. My main issue with this system is that the control scheme for each party
member is a bit different, and their mechanics were designed specifically for
the encounters and systems present in their respective DLC. They did not feel
particularly well integrated with the base game content. Playing as these
characters felt like those weird mods you see pop up from time to time that add
Sekiro combat to Skyrim and stuff like that. It doesn't _actually fit_ because
nothing else around it interacts with it at all. Still, I appreciate them giving
players more options with whatever assets they've got. It's a nice gesture, if
nothing else. I just don't think this is the slam dunk success story that many
other players seem to think it is.

### Exploration

This was somehow worse than combat.

For me, the appeal of an open world _shatters_ the moment there is a goal that
is even slightly urgent. I simply can't suspend my disbelief enough to accept
that these characters with huge responsibilities to get to are just wandering in
random directions. Even worse when some of the things they get up to are clearly
time wasters either for the player or the characters themselves. There needs to
be a very solid narrative justification for that to work for me, and that rarely
happens. It certainly did not happen in this game. It often feels like the open
world and the main story of the game are entirely separate games, stapled
together.

All of the side content was immediately unenjoyable for me. I tried to dabble in
a few parts of it, just to give it a shot, and I found myself quickly wishing it
was over every time. The good news is, if you like this stuff, there's a TON of
it. The length of the game could likely be tripled if not quadrupled for a
player that loves to engage in every possible bit of it.

The most frustrating part was that this side content wasn't just pointless, it
actively fights against itself.

They give you a car, but it can't go off-road, and you essentially just tell it
where to autopilot towards. It's slow fast travel with extra steps. They later
added a way to go off-road, but it's buggy and clearly doesn't fit into the rest
of the game very well---more of a checkbox fix than something that actually
addresses the problem. You can also get chocobo mounts that are much nicer and
better integrated, but for some ridiculous reason they have time limits and
daily upkeep costs. Unnecessary. Including more of the mundane, repetitive tasks
of real life to a fantasy RPG surely makes it better, right?

There's a day/night cycle. It sucks. There's this whole mechanic where your
usual driver can't drive at night because it's simply far too dangerous. So you
must take the wheel. But as soon as you get within a mile of anything dangerous,
the car swerves to a halt and forces you out to address the threat on foot.
Either of these things could have worked alone. Both together are incredibly
stupid.

For a game supposedly about going on a camping/road trip with the boys, this
game sure wants to punish you for doing that. There's a risk/reward XP mechanic:
You only see the benefits of the XP you've accumulated when you sleep. Certain
locations (luxury hotels and the like) give you an XP multiplier. This
encourages you to go as long as you can without resting so that as much of your
XP as possible gets a high multiplier. It is optimal to never camp unless the
game forces you. Yet, a significant amount of narrative content happens at those
camps, and a number of other features only work at camps. You can't have both.

This is only tangentially related to exploration, but there's a skill tree in
the game that causes all of the exploration activities to grant more XP. Sounds
nice, right? It allows players who will engage in that content to benefit from
it more, allowing them to customize their playstyle. Wrong. These skills have
such a miniscule benefit that even if you do all exploration activities in the
game with them active, they won't even pay off their own costs. These skills
effectively only light your skill points on fire. The only gameplay mod I
downloaded was one that corrected this very silly mistake by making ths skills
cheap. They still collectively maybe earned me only one extra skill point over
the course of my entire playthrough. Even funnier, they don't work at all for
the latter two thirds of the game, because only the initial area is open-world
and explorable.

## Story

I really, really wish I had more to say about this. Over six months of thinking
about this, off and on, I didn't come up with any interesting thoughts about
this game's plot. It's one of the Final Fantasy plots of all time. Very easily
the worst one. Even the games I overall enjoyed less than this one at least had
better writing. Even FFII.

Whatever this story was trying to say, it got lost somewhere along the line
during the repeated changes in management, compression of multiple games into
one, rampant product placement, and market-driven decision making. This game is
a shell of what it could have been, and that makes me very sad.

Trying to make sense of this story feels like being repeatedly blindfolded, spun
in circles until made dizzy, and then set free in a random direction. It's
disorienting and sudden. The previous goal will be discarded like trash, only to
be replaced with an even more ephemeral goal. There are like 17 different arcs
that are all blended and jumbled together without any resolution or transition.

One of the most annoying parts was how party members would randomly disappear
from the story to go on their DLC adventures, and then return to show you the
results of those adventures in order to get you to go buy the DLC for context.
This already confusing and poorly paced game further sabotaged its own pacing
and narrative integrity, to bully players into spending more money. This is the
video game equivalent of "I'm going to shit myself in front of you if you don't
pay me $20."

## External Content and DLC

I was originally so excited for this retrospective that I planned on reading the
book that comes after the game, detailing the DLC stories that were cancelled. I
did not even start it. I did, however, play all the DLC, watch the movie, and
play the spin off game A King's Tale.

### A King's Tale

Lets start with that last one. This small game is a side-scrolling beat-em-up
released as promotional material for the game. It's only a couple hours long,
and covers some of King Regis' backstory and the events leading up to the game.

There are some characters in FFXV that are presented with a certain emphasis
that indicates the player should find their appearance significant somehow---
that the player should recognize them and be excited about this reveal. The
entire reason for that is this game. Those characters appeared hear for a small
bit of time each. They are not actually significant. The reveal is nothing
special. It's all just silly marketing done by people who have never actually
played a videogame in their lives. It's Marvel cameo marketing, but even more
forced.

The game itself is fine. Actually, probably the best of this content. The combat
manages to actually be slightly more complex and interesting than the main game.
Still not the most engaging thing ever, but I don't regret spending time on it.
The art style is cool, and the music is solid. I have no problems with this
game. It's especially good for what it is: a playable ad.

### Kingsglaive

This movie was also... fine. It's a pretty standard action movie without too
much interesting going on. Of course, if you truly want to understand the
complete context of this game, you _need_ to watch it. There is a section of the
game that essentially just plays an ad for this movie, and then cuts to the part
of the story after it---assuming you paused the game and watched it immediately.

What frustrated me was that the actors for the movie were different from the
game's actors. I don't think that was necessary. I doubt many people were going
to see this movie who weren't fans of the game, no matter how many big-name
actors were crammed in there. So this move only wasted money, denied the
hard-working game actors more work and money, and made the experience worse for
fans. Truly baffling.

I will give this movie credit for the action scenes, at least. If that's all you
care about, it's an entertaining ride. There are creative ideas in there.

### DLC

Unfortunately, I didn't care for any of the DLC. It's all so transparently
content invented purely for the purposes of becoming DLC. It's not even like
they took important parts out of the story so they could sell them as DLC, they
first invented bullshit story moments to become DLC, injected them into the
story at important points, and then ripped them back out again. Overall, a truly
terrible process that produced nothing of value.

The first time I played this game, I waited until the end of the base game to
play all the DLC. This time, I stopped the main story to go play the DLC
whenever it logically happened in the story. I hoped that this would help
connect everything together better and improve the pacing. It did not.

I found this content so empty and meaningless that I don't feel like talking
about them any more than that.

# Final Thoughts

**Rating: 5/10**  
_Playtime: 30 hours_

I got progressively angrier as I wrote this review. I am extremely disappointed
in how poorly this game was handled. In my opinion, this was Square Enix at
their very lowest as a company. Things are thankfully looking better these days.
I was excited to reach this point of the retrospective journey, but now that I'm
here I want nothing more than to put it behind me. This will forever be the
Final Fantasy game that makes me the most sad---not because of the story
contained within it, but because of the story of its creation.
